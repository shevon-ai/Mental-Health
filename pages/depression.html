Depression

Your mood and ability to function may be affected by the medical illness known as depression. Clinical depression, bipolar depression, dysthymia, seasonal affective disorder, and other kinds of depression are among them. Counseling, medicine, brain stimulation, and alternative treatments are all available as treatment options.


What is Depression?

Depression is a medical condition that affects your mood and ability to function.

Feeling depressed might entail sadness, anxiety, or hopelessness. Additionally, the illness may make it difficult to think, remember, eat, and sleep. You must experience these feelings on a daily basis for at least two weeks in order to be diagnosed with severe depressive disorder (clinical depression), which also includes other symptoms such trouble sleeping, a lack of interest in activities, or changes in eating.

Depression may worsen and linger longer if left untreated. In extreme circumstances, it may result in death or self-harm. Fortunately, therapies can significantly reduce depressive symptom severity.


Who is at risk for depression?

Nobody is immune to depression, regardless of their age, gender, or situation. Depression affects over 16 million Americans each year.

Depression may affect women more frequently than it does males. Additionally, having at least one depressed episode in your lifetime is more likely if you have certain medical problems or genetics.


What are the types of depression?

Symptoms and causes are used by healthcare professionals to designate different forms of depression. The causes of these occurrences are frequently obscure. They can remain far longer in some persons than in others for no apparent cause.

Types of depression include:

  • Major depressive disorder (MDD): The symptoms of major depression (clinical depression) are severe or overpowering and last for more than two weeks. Everyday living is hampered by these symptoms.

  • Bipolar depression: Bipolar illness patients experience manic and depressed mood swings on a regular basis. They could experience depressive symptoms including sadness, hopelessness, or lack of energy at this time.

  • Perinatal and postpartum depression: "Perinatal" refers to the time immediately before delivery. This kind is sometimes referred to as postpartum depression. Perinatal depression can happen at any time throughout pregnancy or even up to a year after giving birth. The "baby blues" are only one set of symptoms and only result in mild melancholy, anxiety, or stress.

  • Persistent depressive disorder (PDD): Another name for PDD is dysthymia. PDD symptoms are milder than those of major depression. However, PDD symptoms might last for up to two years in some people.

  • Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD): A severe type of premenstrual disorder is premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMS). Women are impacted in the days or weeks before to their period.

  • Psychotic depression: Psychotic depression is characterized by severe depressed symptoms as well as hallucinations or delusions. Hallucinations entail seeing, hearing, or feeling touched by things that aren't truly there, whereas delusions are beliefs in things that aren't grounded in reality.

  • Seasonal affective disorder (SAD): Seasonal depression, often known as SAD, typically begins in late autumn or early winter. In the spring and summer, it frequently disappears.
